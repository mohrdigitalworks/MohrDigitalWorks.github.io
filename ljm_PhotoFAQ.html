Why are my indoor pictures ORANGE?
    Indoor pictues under standard lighting have an orange/red color. Most modern cameras use AUTO COLOR BALANCE to attempt to correct for different colors of light in a scene. However, this is not perfect and sometimes produces an ORANGE cast. Most cameras allow you to set the WHITE BALANCE from AUTO to TUNGSTEN or INCANDESCENT and this should remove the color cast.
NOTE: make sure to RESET the WHITE BALANCE back to AUTO or you will have problems with outdoor photos.
 
Why are my outdoor picture deep BLUE?
    You have probably set the camera's WHITE BALANCE to TUNGSTEN or INCANDESCENT to remove the ORANGE from an indoor scene.
This causes any outdoor scenes to appear BLUE. Reset the camera's WHITE BALANCE to AUTO and this should be corrected.

Why do some of my prints have part of the image cropped off?
    Short answer: The default setting when printing is to fill the print with the image. If the image is a different proportion from the print, some of the image
is cropped off. For example, if your image is SQUARE, you would find some of the top and bottom of the image cropped off of a 4x6" print..
To correct this you need to use a special setting when ordering the print. Please contact us for instructions on using FIT instead of FILL when making prints.


Photo TIP
Always preview images at 100% if you want to see check image quality.
At any zoom ratio other than 100% you are seeing the INTERPRETATION of the viewing program, not the real quality of your image!


Should I keep a copy of my images?
    YES! It is recommended that you have THREE copies of your images. ONE of these copies should be somewhere NOT ON THE SAME PREMISES as the other TWO. After some research it is my opinion that you should make NEW backup copies of your photos about every TWO years. Reguardless of whether you are backing up on CD/.DVD/bluray, Flash drive or Hard drives, media can fail. Even if you backup in the "Cloud" you cannont be sure that your photos will be accessable years from now. With negatives, slides and prints, backing up was expensive, with digital media backups are relatively cheap.

What is IMAGE STABILIZATION ?
    This is a camera feature that attempts to reduce the loss of sharpness in an image due to camera movement.
Since cameras are no longer held against the face, they are more succeptable to CAMERA SHAKE.
Each camera maker has their own term for this feature.
VIBRATION REDUCTION - IMAGE STABILIZATION - SHAKE REDUCTION - OPTICAL IMAGE STABILIZATION (OIS)

I color correct my images before I bring them in but the prints don't look the same. What am I doing wrong?
    This simple question is one of the greatest headaches in digital imaging. In the old days of negatives you didn't have an expectation of how your prints would look because if you looked at your negatives, you couldn't tell what they would look like. With slides you had a positive image to look at but your prints still looked different.
    Today, everyone has seen some version of their digital image on a monitor and wonders why the prints don't match!
    The answer is extremely complex but I will try to keep it as simple as possible.
- an old style CRT screen produced color by glowing phosphors.
- a LCD screen has colored filters lit by a florescent backlite.
- an injet printer produces color using dyes or pigments sprayed onto paper viewed by reflected light.
- a photographic paper print has dyes in an emulsion layer on the paper viewed by reflected light.
All these display methods have different "color spaces" - which simply means thay they each display a different subset of the possible colors.
In order to have the best chance of having what you see on your monitor appear in the print is to "Calibrate" your monitor.
This does NOT match the monitor to the print. What is does is to set the color , brightness and contrast of your monitor to a set STANDARD so you have the best chance of a print made from your image file looking close to your monitor. A Calibrated device should have the widest range of grayscale viewable with little color cast (ie the grays should be a clean neutral gray without a color cast). The contrast and brightness should be optimal for that device.

My software says that my image is 8"x10", why does it not make a good 8"x10" when you print it?
    Any software that says your image will print or "IS" a certain print size is misleading you. A digital image only has a "size" when it is printed, and that size depends on the "DPI" or "Dots Per Inch" at which the output device prints or displays.
    for example, take an image that is 1200pixels by 1800pixels and display it or print it with NO RESIZING.
    On your display which is probably 72DPI, it would display as about 16.6"x25" so you would only see a small part of the image on your screen.
    On our Frontier printer at 300DPI, it would make a nice 4"x6" print.
    On an inkjet printer it would likely print as an 8"x12" print.
   
    Also, remember that when you look at an image on your monitor, you are seeing the display software's interpretation of the image unless you are viewing at 100% zoom.

What resolution should I use when scanning?
     The answer to this question is quite complex. It depends on what media you are scanning from and what you want to do with the resulting image file.
        For prints you can usually simply scan at 300DPI and you should get an image that will print at the same size as the original.
        For negatives and slides you neet much higher resolution. When I am scanning I always set the scan for the size of the image I want to end up with.
        For example:
                I have a 35mm slide ( 1"x1.5") and I want to make 8x10" prints.
                Excellent quality results are usually obtained by printing at 300DPI ( in fact, this is the resolution that our big printer at work uses), so 8x300=2400 pixels by 10x300=3000 pixels. I would set the output size of the scan to be 2400x3000 pixels. This just scratches the surface of the complex art of scanning.
